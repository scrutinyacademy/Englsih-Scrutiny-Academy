UNIT 1 — Personality Development
3 Lessons • Theme: Attitude, Perseverance, Desire for Success
June
Essay / Article

A — Attitude Is Altitude

Nick Vujicic
🌟 Inspirational Disability → Triumph Born: Melbourne, Australia
  • Nick Vujicic was born with no arms and no legs — a rare condition called Phocomelia
  • Has a small foot on his left hip — he calls it his "chicken drumstick" — helps him type, write, balance
  • His parents did not send him to a special school — kept him in mainstream school (hard but best decision)
  • At age 6 — father taught him to type with his toe; mother made a special device to hold a pen
  • At age 13 — read about a disabled man who helped others → decided to encourage and inspire people
  • Won Australian Young Citizen of the Year (1990) for bravery and perseverance
  • Learnt surfing from Bethany Hamilton (who lost her arm to a shark)
  • Famous quote: "If I fail, I try again, and again, and again."
🎯 Central Theme Your attitude determines your altitude in life. Physical disability cannot stop a determined spirit.
Key Quote to Remember: "The challenges in our lives are there to strengthen our convictions. They are not there to run us over."
Biography

B — Every Success Story is Also a Story of Great Failures

APJ Abdul Kalam
🚀 Inspiring Failure → Success Autobiography
  • APJ Abdul Kalam's childhood in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu — poor but disciplined family
  • His greatest dream was to become a pilot in the Indian Air Force — but was rejected (ranked 9th, only 8 selected)
  • Father told him the bitter truth — Kalam was deeply disappointed but did not get angry
  • His classmates went to Madras (Chennai) by train — he went to say goodbye at the station
  • Lesson: Failures are stepping stones to success — every great person has a story of failure
  • Famous people who failed first: Einstein, Edison, Lincoln, Walt Disney — all struggled before succeeding
🎯 Central Theme Failure is not the opposite of success — it is part of the journey. Never give up after failures.

Name → Birth/Place → Education → Career → Achievements → Legacy/Awards

UNIT 2 — Wit and Humour
3 Lessons • Theme: Comedy, Irony, Greed, Cleverness
July
One-Act Play

A & B — The Dear Departed (Part I & II)

Stanley Houghton
Mrs. Amelia Slater Mrs. Elizabeth Jordan Henry Slater Ben Jordan Abel Merryweather (Grandfather) Victoria (Vicky)
  • Setting: The Slater family's house — Mrs. Slater and Mrs. Jordan are sisters
  • Both daughters think grandfather Abel Merryweather is dead — meet to divide his belongings
  • Mrs. Slater tries to shift grandfather's bureau and clock before the Jordans arrive
  • Both families argue about who gets what furniture and who will pay for the funeral
  • Twist: Grandfather wakes up — he was only drunk/asleep, not dead!
  • He overhears their greed and selfishness — he decides to change his will in favour of Mrs. Shorrocks (landlady of pub) to teach his daughters a lesson
  • Humour comes from: dramatic irony — audience knows grandfather is alive, but the family doesn't
🎯 Central Theme Greed and selfishness within families exposed through humour and irony. Money should never come before relationships.
📌 Type of humour: Situational irony + Dramatic irony
📌 Moral: Greedy people get punished. Respect elders while they are alive.
Folk Tale

C — The Brave Potter

Marguerite Siek (retold)
🐯 FolktaleAccidental Bravery
  • A potter loses his donkey and goes searching for it at night, drunk and angry
  • He finds an animal sleeping at an old woman's hut — mistakes a tiger for his donkey
  • He kicks, beats and rides the tiger home and ties it with an iron chain
  • Next morning — villagers see a tiger tied up → think the potter is a great hero
  • The king hears of this "brave" act and sends him to fight a real enemy
  • Humour: the potter is terrified but accidentally wins — the sight of him confuses the enemy soldiers
🎯 Central Theme Sometimes accidents lead to unexpected success. Humour through mistaken identity and misunderstanding.

UNIT 3 — Human Relations
3 Lessons • Theme: Sacrifice, Duty, Women's Issues, Reckless Spending
August
Short Story

A — The Journey

Thakazhi Sivasankara Pillai
💑 Marriage & DutyHilly terrainSacrifice
  • Narrator — a government employee — returns to work after 6 months at home with his newly-wed wife
  • Journey is through hilly terrain with no motorable roads — only foot-paths and footbridges
  • He carries a trunk; the journey is long and difficult
  • His wife secretly follows him for part of the journey — unwilling to let him go alone
  • He sees her from the bus — she waves and turns back — deeply touching moment of love and sacrifice
  • Shows how women quietly sacrifice but their love is immeasurable
🎯 Central Theme Silent, selfless love in human relationships. A woman's devotion expressed not in words but in actions.
Poem

B — Another Woman

Imtiaz Dharker
👩 Women's RightsPatriarchal Society
  • Poem about a woman who wakes up every day and performs her daily duties as a wife and daughter-in-law
  • She cooks, cleans, serves — her identity is defined only by her role in the family
  • Slowly she begins to question — "Who am I beyond these walls?"
  • The poem highlights the suppression of women's identity in a patriarchal society
  • Tone: Initially quiet and resigned → gradually questioning and assertive
She wakes up at dawn
Folds away the dark
Stretches the night out flat...
— (Poem reflects routine suppression of a woman's self)
🎯 Central Theme Women are more than their domestic roles. Society must recognise women's individuality and rights.
One-Act Play

C — The Never-Never Nest

Cedric Mount
Jack (husband) Jill (wife) Aunt Jane Nurse
  • Jack and Jill live a luxurious life — big house, car, piano, radiogram, nurse for baby
  • Aunt Jane visits and is impressed — but is shocked to learn everything is bought on installments/loans
  • They owe money to: hire-purchase company, bank, doctor, nurse, insurance — even the baby's pram is not paid for
  • Aunt Jane warns: "Live within your means" — you'll end up in serious debt
  • Title "Never-Never Nest" = buy-now-pay-later lifestyle (installment = hire-purchase = "never-never")
🎯 Central Theme Reckless spending and living beyond one's means leads to financial disaster. Live within your income.
📌 Key Term: "Never-Never" = Hire Purchase / Installment buying system. The play satirizes the consumerism trap.

UNIT 4 — Films and Theatre
3 Lessons • Theme: Cinema, Art, Legacy of Film-makers

Interview / Profile

A — Rendezvous with Ray

Satyajit Ray (Profile)
🎥 Indian CinemaSatyajit RayBengali filmmaker
  • Satyajit Ray — legendary Indian filmmaker from Kolkata, Bengal
  • Known for realistic, humanistic films — most famous: Pather Panchali (1955) — the Apu trilogy
  • Won Academy Award (Honorary Oscar, 1992) for lifetime achievement
  • Also a writer, illustrator, composer — multifaceted genius
  • His last three films — Ganashatru, Shakha Prashakha, Agantuk — were deeply personal
  • He believed: "It would be a sin to lose faith in Man" — humanist worldview
  • Critics found his last films "didactic" — but Ray was directly speaking his message to society
🎯 Central Theme True art communicates deep human values. Satyajit Ray's legacy shows that cinema can be a powerful medium for social commentary.
Review / Essay

B — Maya Bazaar

Classic Telugu Film (1957)
🎭 Telugu CinemaMythologyClassic Film
  • Maya Bazaar (1957) — considered one of the greatest Telugu mythological films ever made
  • Based on an episode from the Mahabharata — story of Abhimanyu and Sasirekha
  • Famous for its special effects, comedy (Ghatotkacha), and memorable songs
  • The lesson involves a film review and Ramu's letter praising the film
  • Students learn how to write a film review — give opinions backed by specific points
  • Demonstrates how films preserve culture and mythology
📌 Exam Tip: Know how to write a film review — plot, acting, direction, music, message, personal opinion.

UNIT 5 — Social Issues
3 Lessons • Theme: Caste, Discrimination, Family Unity, Loneliness
October
Short Story

A & B — The Storeyed House (Part I & II)

Waman Govind Hoval (Marathi)
🏗️ Caste IssueDalit LiteratureFamily Unity
  • Bayaji and his six sons — from a lower-caste (Dalit) family — decide to build a two-storeyed house
  • In their village, only upper-caste people have storeyed houses — building one is seen as a challenge to caste hierarchy
  • The upper-caste people try to stop them, intimidate them, create obstacles
  • But the six brothers stand united — they dig the foundation with determination
  • The story shows how unity and determination can overcome social discrimination
  • The house becomes a symbol of dignity, equality, and liberation for the Dalit community
🎯 Central Theme Caste discrimination is a deep social evil. Unity within the family and determination can overcome social oppression and inequality.
Key Moment: "We're starting on a house — not one with a concealed first floor but a regular two-storeyed house," — the six brothers resume work with determination.
Poem

Social Poem

  • Connects to the broader social issue of how we treat the elderly in modern society
🎯 Central Theme Society must care for the elderly. Abandoning parents is both a social and moral failure.

UNIT 6 — Bio-Diversity
3 Lessons • Theme: Environment, Nature, Conservation, Pollution
November
Essay / Interview

A — Environment

Wangari Maathai (profile)
🌳 Tree PlantingGreen Belt MovementKenya
  • Wangari Maathai — Kenyan environmentalist, Nobel Peace Prize winner (2004)
  • Founded the Green Belt Movement — mobilised women to plant trees across Kenya
  • Ordinary, illiterate women learned to plant trees → trees gave them income, energy, dignity
  • Key transformation: environmental healing + women's empowerment went hand in hand
  • Her message: Take care of the environment → the environment will take care of you
  • Also discusses threats to biodiversity: deforestation, pollution, toxic waste dumping
🎯 Central Theme Environmental conservation empowers communities. Every individual has a responsibility to protect nature.
Poem

B — Or Will the Dreamer Wake?

Poem on Environment
  • Poem raises an urgent question: will humans wake up before they destroy nature?
  • Images of a dying earth — forests gone, rivers polluted, animals extinct
  • The "dreamer" is humanity — still dreaming of progress while destroying the planet
  • Tone: Warning, urgent, melancholic — a plea to wake up before it is too late
Will the rivers still flow?
Will the forests still grow?
Or will the dreamer wake
When it's too late?
Comparison Essay

C — A Tale of Three Villages

Global Comparison
🌍 Nigeria🌏 Philippines🇮🇳 India
  • Compares three villages across three continents — all affected by environmental/social problems
  • Koko Village, Nigeria: Toxic waste drums dumped illegally — skull & crossbones visible — serious health risk to villagers
  • Philippines village: Deforestation — logging has caused floods and landslides; community is now fighting back by replanting trees
  • Indian village: Traditional water conservation methods — community managing local resources wisely
  • Common thread: Global corporations/human greed damage local ecosystems
  • Solution: Community action, awareness, traditional wisdom can protect biodiversity
🎯 Central Theme Environmental problems are global. Local communities are the first victims — and also the best solution.

UNIT 7 — Nation and Diversity
3 Lessons • Theme: Unity in Diversity, India's Heritage
December
Autobiography

A — My Childhood

APJ Abdul Kalam
🚀 APJ Abdul KalamRameswaramSecularism
  • APJ Abdul Kalam grew up in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu — a small, deeply religious town
  • His family was poor — father was a boat owner (ferried pilgrims to Pamban island)
  • His best friends were Ramanadha Sastry (Hindu), Aravindan, Sivaprakasan — different religions, one friendship
  • Teacher Sivasubramania Iyer — stood against caste discrimination — invited Kalam to eat at his home
  • New teacher asked Kalam to sit separately (as he was Muslim, near Hindu brahmin boy) — Sivasubramania stood up against this
  • Kalam's childhood shows Hindu-Muslim unity, secularism, communal harmony
  • Father Abdul Kalam was respected by all — showed that faith in one's religion doesn't mean intolerance of others
🎯 Central Theme India's strength lies in its unity in diversity — people of different faiths can live as one community. True education creates secular, humane citizens.
📌 Key Message: Kalam's childhood teaches us that religion, caste and community should never divide friendships or human dignity.
Poem

B — A Plea for India

Poem on National Unity
🕊️ UnityPatriotismSecularism
  • Poem makes a passionate appeal for national unity, brotherhood and harmony
  • India has many religions, languages and cultures — yet is ONE nation
  • The poet pleads: rise above narrow divisions of caste, religion, language — be Indian first
  • Tone: emotional, patriotic, hopeful, sincere
Let us be one, though many
Let rivers of difference

Essay

C — Unity in Diversity in India

Informative Essay
  • India has many ethnic groups, languages, religions, customs — yet has existed as one civilisation for thousands of years
  • Common uniting bonds: shared history, geography, Constitution, national symbols
  • Even early Indian history shows that people saw India as a single political unit
  • India is NOT just a geographical expression — it is a living unity of cultures
  • The essay argues that diversity is India's strength, not a weakness
🎯 Central Theme India's diversity is its greatest strength. Unity, not uniformity, is the basis of India's nationhood.

UNIT 8 — Human Rights
3 Lessons • Theme: Racial Equality, Dignity, Identity, Rights
January
Short Story

A — Jamaican Fragment

A.L. Hendricks
🌍 JamaicaRacial PrejudiceChildren's Innocence
  • Narrator — a Black Jamaican man — walks to work every day and watches two boys play
  • One boy is white, one is Black — the white boy is a master, the black boy is a servant
  • Narrator is deeply disturbed by this racial inequality — his faith in humanity is shaken
  • Next day: roles are reversed! The black boy is giving orders, the white boy is following
  • The children play both roles without prejudice — they have not yet learned racism
  • Lesson: Racial prejudice is taught, not natural — children are naturally equal
  • Title "Jamaican Fragment" — a small slice of life that reveals a big truth about racism
🎯 Central Theme All human beings are born equal. Racial discrimination is a man-made evil. Children show us the way — they do not naturally discriminate.
Opening Statement of the Story: "All human beings are born free and equal in dignity and rights."
Poem

B — Once Upon a Time

Gabriel Okara
🧒 InnocenceAfricaLosing naturalness
  • A father speaks to his son — once upon a time, people were genuine and kind
  • Now people wear "ice-block cold eyes", "shake hands without hearts" — society has become artificial
  • The father has learned to fake smiles, fake laughter to fit into society
  • He asks his son to teach him to be genuine again — "teach me how to laugh" from the heart
  • Children's natural, innocent behaviour is the ideal — adults lose it due to social pressure
Once upon a time, son,
they used to laugh with their hearts
and laugh with their eyes:
but now they only laugh with their teeth...
🎯 Central Theme Modern society forces us to become fake and artificial. We must reclaim our natural, innocent humanity — like children.
Personal Narrative

C — What is My Name?

Personal narrative on identity
🏷️ IdentityDignitySelf-respect
  • Narrator reflects on the importance of one's name as a symbol of identity and dignity
  • In colonial or discriminatory settings, people's names were ignored or replaced — a denial of their personhood
  • The story argues that every person has a right to be called by their own name
  • Connects to the broader issue of human dignity — being recognised and respected as an individual
  • Human Rights angle: The right to identity is a fundamental human right
🎯 Central Theme Every person's name, identity and dignity must be respected. Stripping someone of their name is stripping them of their humanity.
📌 Unit 8 Key Idea: All three texts in Unit 8 argue that every human being — regardless of race, gender, or background — has equal rights, dignity, and identity.

Grammar at a Glance
Active → Passive:
O + be + V3 + by + S
Direct → Indirect:
Remove quotes, add "that", change tense + pronouns + time words
Degrees:
Positive: as___as | Comparative: ___er than | Superlative: the ___est
Editing (T·S·A·P·P):
Tense → Subject-Verb → Articles → Prepositions → Pronouns
Synthesis:
Simple (1 clause) / Compound (and,but,so) / Complex (when,because,if)
Too→So:
too + adj + to → so + adj + that + cannot
